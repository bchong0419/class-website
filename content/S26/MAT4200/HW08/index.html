Exercise 3 [10] (Second fundamental form)

Recall that the first fundamental form \( \mathrm{I} \colon T_pM \times T_pM \to \mathbb{R} \) is defined intrinsically as the map \[ \mathrm{I}(a \mathbf{r}_u(p) + b \mathbf{r}_v(p), c \mathbf{r}_u(p) + d \mathbf{r}_v(p)) = acE + (ad+bc)F + bdG, \] where \[ \begin{align*} E = \langle \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_u \rangle, \quad F = \langle \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v \rangle, \quad G = \langle \mathbf{r}_v, \mathbf{r}_v \rangle. \end{align*} \] We typically write this as \[ \mathrm{I} = E du^2 + 2F du\, dv + G dv^2 \] or in the matrix notation (with respect to the basis \(\{ \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v \} \)) \[ \mathrm{I}(\mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2) = \langle \mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = \mathbf{w}_1^T \begin{pmatrix} E & F \\ F & G \end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2. \]

Now the second fundamental form \[ \mathrm{I\!I} \colon T_pM \times T_pM \to \mathbb{R} \] is defined extrinsically with respect to the Gauss map \( \mathbf{N} \colon M \to S^2 \) and its differential \( d\mathbf{N}_p \colon T_pM \to T_{\mathbf{N}(p)}(S^2) \), namely: \[ \mathrm{I\!I}(\mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2) = \langle -d\mathbf{N}_p(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = \langle P(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle. \] Here, \( P = -d\mathbf{N}_p \) is called the shape operator.1

We will prove that with respect to the basis \(\{ \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v \} \), \[ \mathrm{I\!I}(\mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2) = \mathbf{w}_1^T \begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2, \] where \[ L = -\mathbf{r}_{uu} \cdot \mathbf{N}, \quad M = -\mathbf{r}_{uv} \cdot \mathbf{N}, \quad N = -\mathbf{r}_{vv} \cdot \mathbf{N}. \] (Here, \( M \) is a real number, and is different from our surface \( M \).)

Lastly, we will use the first and second fundamental forms (which are easy to compute) to determine the Gaussian and mean curvatures at every point on our surface.

  1. Explain why \( \langle \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{N} \rangle = 0 \).
  2. Take the partial derivative of the inner product with respect to \( u \), and use this to prove that \( \langle \mathbf{N}, \mathbf{r}_{uu} \rangle = \langle P(\mathbf{r}_{u}), \mathbf{r}_{u} \rangle \).
  3. Extend this idea to prove that with respect to the basis \(\{ \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v \} \), \[ \langle P(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = \mathbf{w}_1^T \begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2. \]
  4. Now we want to recover the eigenvalues of the shape operator \( P \). Recall that our inner product is precisely \( \mathrm{I} \), and thus \[ \langle P(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = P(\mathbf{w}_1)^T \begin{pmatrix} E & F \\ F & G \end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2. \] Use this to prove that \[ P(\mathbf{w}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} E & F \\ F & G \end{pmatrix}^{-1}\begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_1. \]
  5. Recall that the Gaussian curvature \( K \) is the product of the eigenvalues of the shape operator (equivalently \( \det(P) \)) and use this to prove that \[ K = \kappa_1\kappa_2 = \frac{LN-M^2}{EG - F^2}. \]
  6. Recall that the mean curvature is the average of the eigenvalues (equivalently \( \frac{1}{2}\operatorname{tr}(P) \)) and use this to prove that \[ \frac{\kappa_1 + \kappa_2}{2} = \frac{EN - 2FM + GL}{2(EG - F^2)}. \]
  7. On our previous homework, you computed the principal curvatures, mean curvature, and Gaussian curvature of the surface \( M \) given by the parametric equation \[ \mathbf{r}(u,v) = \begin{pmatrix} u \\ v \\ uv\end{pmatrix},\quad (u,v) \in (-1,1) \times (-1, 1), \] at the point \( p = \mathbf{r}(0,0) \). Now compute \( E \), \( F \), \( G \), \( L \), \( M \), and \( N \) and use this to compute the mean curvature, Gaussian curvature, and principal curvatures. (Computing the first and second fundamental forms at each point is better than computing \( dN \) directly, right?)

Let \( C \) be a curve on a surface \( S \subset \mathbb{R}^3 \), and let \( \kappa \) be the curvature of \( C \) at \( p \). If \( \mathbf{N} \in \mathbb{R}^3 \) is the normal vector of \( C \), \( \mathbf{n} \) is the vector normal to the surface \( S \) at \( p \), and \( \theta \) is the angle between \( \mathbf{N} \) and \( \mathbf{n} \). The normal curvature of \( C \subset S \) at \( p \) is defined as \[ \kappa_n = \kappa \cos \theta \]

The surprising fact about this definition is that if we have two curves \( \gamma_1(t_0) = \gamma_2(s_0) = p \) and \( \gamma'_1(t_0) \) is parallel to \( \gamma'_2(s_0) \), then their normal curvatures agree at \( p \). (!!)

Let \( \mathbf{r}(u,v) = \begin{pmatrix} u \\ u v \\ u+v \end{pmatrix} \). Describe two different curves on the surface that go through the point \( p = (0, 0, 1) \) such that the velocities of the two curves are scalar multiples of each other at \( p \), and the curvatures at that point are different. Include a plot of how these look on the surface.

Compute the normal curvatures of these curves at \( p \) in \( S \) and check that they agree.

Extra Credit Exercise 8 [8] (Plotting curvature – Programming)

You can color a parametric plot in Mathematica using the ColorFunction option like this: 

ParametricPlot3D[f[u, v], {u, 0, 1}, {v, -1, 2},
  ColorFunctionScaling -> False,
  ColorFunction -> Function[{x, y, z, u, v}, ...]
]
Write Mathematica code (and don't just use a built-in) to determine the curvature as a function of \( u \) and \( v \), and then fill in the ... shown above to make the color of the point on the surface correspond to its curvature.

Give at least three examples of interesting surfaces, and include two images of each: one colored according to the Gaussian curvature, and one corresponding to the mean curvature.

As a hint, something you could do is compute the minimum and maximum curvature over the region, Kmin and Kmax, and replace ... in the above code with something like 

ColorData["TemperatureMap"][

]
which scales the Gaussian curvature to fit ColorData["TemperatureMap"], which takes inputs in the range \( [0, 1] \).
