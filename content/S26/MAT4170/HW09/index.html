Choice Exercise 7 [8] (Burnside's lemma)

This problem will have you solve a natural problem in combinatorics where you will count something that I don't think anyone has ever counted before: how many ways are there to fill the diagram for the $n$-th centered square number (illustrated below) with tiles that consist of either a horizontal or vertical stripe: and

The diagram for the \( n \)-th centered square number can be defined by placing unit squares centered at all points \( (x, y) \in \mathbb{Z}^2 \) satisfying \( |x| + |y| < n \). Let \( a(n) \) be the number of ways of tiling the diagram for the \( n \)-th centered square number up to the symmetries of \( D_4 \), which are the rotations and reflections of the square.

  1. Prove by induction or any other method that the number of squares in the diagram is $n^2 + (n-1)^2$, and describe the diagram for the first centered square number.
  2. Compute $a(1)$ by "brute-force" by drawing all of the possible ways of tiling the square up to rotation/reflection.
  3. Compute \( a(2) \) using Burnside's lemma. (Note, you might just do the next part first.)
  4. Give a formula for \( a(n) \) using Burnside's lemma, and check that it specializes to give the correct value for \( a(1). \)
  5. Compute explicit numerical values for \( a(3) \), \( a(4) \), and \( a(5) \). (Using a calculator like WolframAlpha might be helpful here!)
  6. $a(n) \neq 2^{n^2 + (n-1)^2 - 3}$, but it's an "asymptotically close" approximation. What is the relative error between $a(5)$ and the estimation? Which is bigger, \( a(5) \) or \( 2^{n^2 + (n-1)^2 - 3} \)?
  7. Look up the sequence \( a(1), a(2), a(3), a(4), a(5) \) on the On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer sequences, and see if it's there!
